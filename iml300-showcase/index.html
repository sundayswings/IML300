Project 2: HyperNarrative

walking all day in suzhou

The website can be seen as a self-reflexive walk in a township that lies on the verge of the industrial area of Suzhou. My cousin’s family live there and I used to visit them during New Year, so that’s why I was able to wander around in this particular area. The area is so mesmerizing to me, its vibrancy, its inclusiveness, its quality of everyday life, etc, which makes the place seem not yet severely influenced by the government’s modernizing project. However, the area has been recently incorporated as part of the larger industrial area with an apparent intent to develop it. This makes me feel more urgent in documenting the place.

I hope to demonstrate the place’s vibrancy on the ground while also questioning my standpoint as a city-dweller obviously not content with the modernizing discourse and thus seeking comfort and relief when strolling in this place. In other words, I hope this project shows traces of me looking for ways to express my personal affection toward this place as well as my reservation in seeing it as a spectacle and a vessel that contains my anxiety.

Project 3: Networked Justice

Not the Eternal Name

For this website project, I hope to demonstrate the diverse variations of surnames in the Sinophone world (i.e. the Chinese-speaking world). Integral to the concept of ‘Sinophone’ is to de-center China and Mandarin while rendering the consistently marginalized derivatives of the “Chinese” visible, being their cultures, languages, writings, etc. I chose to focus on a tiny snippet of the Sinophone word - surnames - to hopefully open up a starting point for interested audiences to get a sense of the fuzzy intricacy in the Sinophone world.

“The name that can be named is not the eternal name (名可名，非常名)”. I decided to use the “not the eternal name” for my website in alignment with the concept of sinophone - to argue against sino-centrism, a hegemonic standard way to spell, to pronounce. Moreover, one can also read “not the eternal name” as a caution not to jump to a quick conclusion on each individual’s life trajectory. After all, the surname is just a starting point for understanding the heterogeneity of individuals in the diaspora.

The surnames are manifested in two major ways. First, it is a transliteration of their mother tongue dialects/languages to English alphabet. Second, the surnames are sometimes localized/re-invented/de-sinicized in response to the new country’s socio-political environment. I did not nor I could collect all variations of surnames and map dialects/regions onto them. Yet I hope to at least deliver the fuzziness and intricacy.

** Thoughts **

1 The reading traces the development of computer art with a gendered perspective. It’s my first time seriously reading about computer art/digital art that it seems to me a whole new temporality to explore, perhaps alongside the thread of contemporary art in East Asia. In the reading, I find this quote quite lively and concise to describe computer art - “We talk about form and colour like other artists, but our ‘words’ are brush strokes and software, colour and math…” - and I also see it responding to, or destabilizing, a prevalently perceived dichotomy of art and technology, in which the reading exemplifies through the case of the Artist Lillian Schwartz. As technology in her time embodied masculinities such as the military, Schwartz's practice of using computers in her art faced the stigma of losing the purity reserved (only) in traditional art form. The reading also brings up a historical moment in which the conventional gendered division of labor was harnessed in a magazine, that women are naturally attentive to details, to justify and encourage women’s participation in computer programming. This sort of reminds me that the typists were predominantly women in the 80s and 90s in China.
2 The “Black Gooey Universe” reading provides a decolonizing perspective on how to view GUI-driven technology in relation to blackness, particularly during the old days of Silicon Valley. The icons and graphics that are readily taken for granted as seemingly apolitical visual hints or aesthetics are examined as social constructs imbued with ideology, powerful enough to shape our expectation of what’s supposed to appear on the screen (in this case, a dichotomy of blackness and white neutrality). It interests me that the representation on a computer screen could be approached from a colored perspective. I really appreciate the author’s poetic flow of words about what blackness can be on page 42 - a cacophony of conflicting states yet inherently human. Overall, as I myself start to learn to code and am compelled to think about designs, the GUI that I’m so used to that it seems to exist before I start to destabilize, prompting me to at least think about different possibilities of visuality.
3 I really enjoyed reading Jenny Odell’s How to Internet. Perhaps now out of an angst and nostalgia for her past experiences with the Internet, Jenny writes to trace the Internet’s potentiality for informality and spontaneity, or namely those random virtual encounters with regular people all around the world. It seems like a decentralized world without much mediation from the commercial and the algorithms. As an avid Internet ‘surfer’ myself, I do resonate with the author on her excitement and surprise when accidentally stumble upon things that are mundane but interesting. The article reminds me of how Internet is actually a much wider space to explore and interact with and that there is no singular way of how to Internet.
4 The art projects exemplified by Lauren McCarthy during the talk seem to me a parallel to Jenny Odell’s How to Internet that we read in week 4. They are inherently interested in the sense of spontaneity and authenticity in human connection virtually and physically. A perhaps collective experience of alienation and disbelief in the atomization of individuals prompts us to be curious about strangers that we pass by in the city. To people watch, to mobilize one’s imagination of others based on our sense, to rethink our relationship with our nearby, yet the urge to be noticed, be seen, be understood. It reminds me of how we are all social animals varying in extent.
5 The reading “In Defense of Poor Image” prompts us to contemplate and challenge the popularly perceived aesthetic of cinema and the seemingly unchallengeable righteousness in defending copyrights. It is not a total celebration of poor image inverting the western hegemony for popular consumption though, but an invitation to inspect poor images and their consumers as subjects. Poor images are able to travel to those areas unattended/igored by the Center, areas short of distribution infrastructure and barred from proper circulation due to political constraints. “[I]t is propelled onto new and ephemeral screens stitched together by the desires of dispersed spectators” (43). They have tremendous potential for empowerment and democracy while ambivalently jeopardizing copyrights. On the other hand, poor image also correlates to imperfect cinema that with technology such as dv and mobile phone become increasingly accessible, film-making also becomes an art for the mass, whose standards and aesthetic should no longer be determined by the niche of professionally trained, perhaps orthodox people.
6 The Ted talk is a good reminder to us that what’s behind the technology is still human, inevitably a carrier of bias embedded in the power structures. And yet from a user’s perspective, technology seems quite authoritative to me as it abstracts its mechanism from users’ vision and its smell of tech-savvy niche could bar users from challenging it. The talk is inspirational in the sense that Joy Buolamwini as both the consumer and creator of technology shared her personal story that made her aware of such embedded biases.
7 The TED talk by Chimamanda Adichie aims to elicit the process of enculturation embedded in our everyday life, something as minute as reading some stories before sleep, that contributes and reinforces certain biases we carry. Growing up, I was never instructed nor encouraged to think critically about the selection of articles, books, films, news pieces, etc. that I was either required or recommended to engage at school. It was therefore empowering to start thinking about questions like “why are we taught this but not that”, an effort to destabilize the perceived stasis of syllabus. And such framework of singularity is just so generalizable to the broader socio-political milieu.
8 Christine Sun Kim’s performance (I found the word “performance” better capture the entirety of her TED “talk”) reminds me of how our human body is not to be viewed simply as a passive recipient of cultural imprints, but as the progressing, self-developable means to express and interact with the environment. Kim’s perspective as a hearing-impaired individual in this audio-centric society is particularly valuable. Sounds, as the supposedly primary way in which most people navigate their lives, are also to be “felt, seen, and experienced as an idea”, despite its audio-ality, as embodied in the movements of fingers, facial expression, speed, etc. Kim made a beautiful, yet experientially painful in this audio-centric world, analogy between visual languages and music. Similar to a chord where multiple factors such as speed, strength, and use of fingers all play a crucial role in determining the sentiments, the bodily movements mobilized to convey ideas are inherently nuanced and vivid. What touches me deep down is Kim’s energy and her keen consciousness of her own agency as a hearing-impaired woman, that what if these painful wounds are not to trap, but an invitation to open up a whole new world of sensitivity.
